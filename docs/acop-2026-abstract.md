# From Literature Data to Joint Posterior: Submodel Decomposition and Relevance-Weighted Inference for Quantitative Systems Pharmacology Calibration

## Objectives

Quantitative systems pharmacology models require calibration of dozens to hundreds of parameters from heterogeneous data sources. Established approaches — including optimization-based fitting, Latin hypercube sampling, and manual literature curation — have proven effective for generating plausible parameterizations, but face challenges when integrating data of varying quality, propagating uncertainty consistently, and capturing correlations among jointly informed parameters. We present a two-stage Bayesian calibration framework that addresses these gaps by treating the joint posterior distribution as the central object of inference, complementing existing calibration workflows with principled uncertainty quantification and multi-source data integration.

## Methods

In Stage 1, literature-derived quantitative claims are encoded as structured specifications connecting extracted measurements to self-contained forward models (algebraic relationships or small systems of ordinary differential equations) involving subsets of model parameters. Each data source carries a translation sigma, a composite uncertainty term computed from an 8-axis relevance rubric (indication match, species, measurement directness, source quality, etc.) that is applied inside the likelihood to downweight less translatable evidence — a challenge typically handled through manual judgment in standard workflows. The parameter-target graph decomposes into sparse connected components (typically 1-10 parameters each), enabling exact Markov chain Monte Carlo inference (No-U-Turn Sampler) per component without the approximations required for high-dimensional sampling. Hierarchical partial pooling shares information across biologically related parameters, offering a principled alternative to borrowing point estimates across analogous processes. The resulting per-component posteriors are represented as parametric marginal distributions joined by a Gaussian copula that preserves within-component correlations. In Stage 2, this joint posterior serves as an informative prior for simulation-based inference (neural posterior estimation) against clinical endpoints requiring the full simulator — bridging the gap between literature-informed priors and full-model calibration that is often navigated ad hoc.

## Results

We demonstrate the framework on a mechanistic immuno-oncology model of pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma, calibrating immune cell densities, trafficking rates, and cytokine concentrations from published clinical and preclinical data. The audit pipeline produces automated diagnostics — posterior contraction, cross-target conflict detection, convergence metrics, and posterior predictive checks — providing systematic quality assessment that is difficult to achieve when parameters are estimated through disparate methods. Contraction metrics identify which data sources meaningfully constrain each parameter, while conflict detection flags inconsistencies across sources that might otherwise go unnoticed in manual curation.

## Conclusions

By structuring calibration as explicit Bayesian inference, this framework extends current practice with reproducible uncertainty quantification, formalized data quality weighting, and correlation-aware joint estimation. The resulting posterior serves as both a standalone calibration and a composable prior for downstream clinical fitting, offering a unified probabilistic workflow that builds on — rather than replaces — existing domain expertise in parameter selection.
